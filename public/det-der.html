When to use or “there” and “that”?

"It's nice weather today"

"There are a pair of gloves in the car"

"There/it's almost 20 degrees and it's not windy at all"

"There" and "that" are used as (formal/provisional) subjects in these kinds of general sentences. I can't find a concrete rule that we can relate to. If we instead look in the Nudan dictionary for differences, we can read the following.

DER (formal subject)

"There" takes the place of the subject in the sentence when the actual subject does not have this position, or when there is no actual subject.

Examples: "there is money in the till" • "beer will arrive soon" • "there were life and happy days" • "there was trouble immediately" • "now there is no more" • "it was very dark in the forest.

THERE (adverb)

Refers to a specific place where the speaker is not himself.

Examples: "there's the diet" • "that flower is beautiful" • "he's coming!"

THAT (provisional subject, and subject in statements about the weather)

Examples: "it is easy to read the capital letters" • "it was Per who came in the door" • "it was the dog that had eaten the meat bone" • "it looks like we're going to have an election soon” • “it's hot” • “it's cold” • “it's raining” • “it's snowing” • “it's storming”

THERE OR THAT?

The underlined example is interesting, because here I would think that you can say both

"It was very dark inside the forest" and

"It was very dark inside the forest."

The difference (and it is possibly a linguistic nuance) lies in the fact that "there" is more connected to the forest: "In there in the forest..." " It" seems less connected to the forest - and more connected to the state of darkness.

Whether the same applies in relation to your sentence, I have more doubts. But "there" could, for example, refer to outside or somewhere else: "It's almost 20 degrees (outside/in there/in the basement)", where "it" is more of a general statement of the temperature in the style of the other weather statements (the raining/winding etc.)

"There" and "it" cannot thus be used interchangeably, but in some cases it seems to be linguistic nuances that determine whether the choice should be one or the other.

Der eller det? af Ivan Markvardt | 15. jan 2019 | Spørg om sproget Hvornår man bruger hhv. “der” og “det”? “Det er dejligt vejr i dag” “Der ligger et par handsker i bilen” “Der/det er næsten 20 grader, og det blæser slet ikke” “Der” og “det” bruges som (formelt/foreløbigt) subjekt i den slags generelle sætninger. Jeg kan ikke finde en konkret regel, vi kan forholde os til. Hvis vi i stedet kigger i Nudansk ordbog efter forskelle, kan vi læse følgende. DER (formelt subjekt) “Der” står på subjektets plads i sætningen, når det egentlige subjekt ikke har denne placering, eller når der ikke er noget egentligt subjekt. Eksempler: “der er penge i kassen” • “der kommer snart øl” • “der var liv og glade dage” • “der blev straks ballade” • “nu er der ikke mere” • “der var meget mørkt inde i skoven.” DER (adverbium) Henviser til et bestemt sted, som den talende ikke selv er på. Eksempler: “der står kosten” • “den blomst der er smuk” • “der kommer han jo!” DET (foreløbigt subjekt, og subjekt i udsagn om vejret) Eksempler: “det er let at læse de store bogstaver” • “det var Per, der kom ind ad døren” • “det var hunden, der havde spist kødbenet” • “det ser ud, som om vi snart får valg” • “det er varmt” • “det er koldt” • “det regner” • “det sner” • “det stormer”. DER ELLER DET? Det understregede eksempel er interessant, for her vil jeg mene, at man kan sige både “Der var meget mørkt inde i skoven” og “Det var meget mørkt inde i skoven”. Forskellen (og det er muligvis en sproglig nuance) ligger i, at “der” knytter sig mere til skoven: “Derinde i skoven …” “Det” virker mindre knyttet til skoven – og mere knyttet til tilstanden af mørke. Om det samme gør sig gældende i forhold til din sætning, er jeg mere i tvivl om. Men “der” kunne fx henvise til udenfor eller et andet sted: “Der er næsten 20 grader (uden for/derinde/i kælderen)”, hvor “det” nærmere er en generel konstatering af temperaturen i stil med de øvrige vejrudsagn (det regner/blæser osv.). “Der” og “det” kan således ikke bruges i flæng, men i nogle tilfælde lader det til at være sproglige nuancer, der afgør, om valget skal være det ene eller det andet.
